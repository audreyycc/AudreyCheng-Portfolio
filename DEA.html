Dielectric Elastomer Actuator (DEA)

Soft Pump

Summer 2024

Project Description

This project was conducted in the Clarke Lab at Harvard's School of Engineering and Applied Science and advised by Professor David R. Clarke. The project was completed through the Program for Research in Science and Engineering at Harvard College during the summer of 2024. I am currently developing the final design further to work with the liquid metal alloy eGaIn.

Abstract

Published in the HSURV 2023 Abstract Book

Soft robotics provides flexibility and adaptability ideal for creating versatile, responsive technologies suited for use in contact with human beings. The objective of this project is to develop a soft pump using a design for dielectric elastomer actuators (DEAs) we previously developed, which morphs into a hemispherical shape in response to an applied voltage. The multilayer DEAs are constructed by spin coating CN9028 acrylic elastomer onto an acrylic substrate followed by stamping carbon nanotubes over the elastomer using circular PET masks of varying diameters, repeating these steps to form five layers. The pump structure is constructed by pouring elastomer into 3D printed molds, using additional elastomer to attach both sides of the mold and DEAs together. Initial pump designs failed to induce uniaxial flow when using Tesla valves to control the flow, likely due to the size of the liquid channels, slow flow rate, and structure material. We removed the Tesla valves and modified the design to use multiple DEAs arranged sequentially as valves. The modified structure uses two outer DEAs which actuate downward in sequence into the fluid channel to control the liquid flow direction. A central DEA acts as a pump. Using LabView to program the DEAs to actuate in specific sequences, the pump moves liquid uniaxially with partial success, though the efficiency of fluid movement is currently inconsistent. The interaction between DEAs has been identified as a critical factor affecting the systemâ€™s performance. Current considerations to improve the pump include varying the thickness of the DEAs, number of diode layers in each DEA, and the distances between the DEAs on the pump structure to enhance unidirectional flow and overall reliability. Successful development of the soft pump will allow for its integration into a tunable antenna system using the liquid metal alloy eGaIn, which has applications in wearable electronics and medical devices
